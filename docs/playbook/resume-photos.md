# Photos on Resumes

Including a photo on your resume is one of the few formatting decisions that can actively hurt your chances. The research is rich but geographically uneven, and it's important to be honest about where the evidence comes from.

## The evidence gap: why US/UK photo studies barely exist

Almost all large-scale resume photo audit studies come from countries where CV photos are the norm: Israel, Argentina, Italy, Germany. In the US and UK, photos on resumes are so uncommon that researchers don't test them. US correspondence studies signal race through names instead, most famously [Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004)](https://nber.org/papers/w9873), who sent fictitious resumes to help-wanted ads in Boston and Chicago and found white-sounding names received **50% more callbacks** than Black-sounding names.

The methodological literature explains the gap explicitly: photos bundle attractiveness, ethnicity, age, weight, and other traits into a single signal, making it impossible to isolate which characteristic drives the screener's decision. US researchers use names because they're a cleaner experimental instrument.

This means the specific callback numbers below come from non-US/UK markets. They demonstrate real mechanisms of bias, but the exact magnitudes may not transfer directly to American or British hiring contexts.

## The beauty premium (and penalty)

The landmark photo study is [Ruffle & Shtudiner (2015)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/mnsc.2014.1927), published in _Management Science_. They sent **5,312 CVs** in pairs to 2,656 job openings in **Israel** (where CV photos are standard). Each pair had one CV with no photo and one with a photo of either an attractive or plain-looking candidate.

**For men**, attractiveness paid off straightforwardly. Attractive men received a **19.9% callback rate** versus **13.7% for plain-looking men** and 9.2% for no-photo CVs.

**For women**, the results flipped. Women with no photo had _significantly higher_ callback rates than both attractive and plain-looking women. The researchers investigated several explanations and concluded the primary driver was **female jealousy among screeners**, since 93% of the HR personnel who handled initial screening were women.

[López Bóo, Rossi & Urzúa (2013)](https://econpapers.repec.org/article/eeeecolet/v_3a118_3ay_3a2013_3ai_3a1_3ap_3a170-172.htm), published in _Economics Letters_, sent **2,540 fictitious CVs** to job openings in **Buenos Aires, Argentina**. They manipulated facial proportions (eye-to-mouth and eye-to-eye distance ratios) to create attractive and unattractive versions. Attractive photos received **36% more callbacks** than unattractive ones.

[Busetta, Fiorillo et al. (2020)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40888-020-00194-5) sent **9,680 CVs** to **Italian** firms and found that gender gaps in callbacks widened further when combined with attractiveness and non-native ethnicity, especially for higher-skilled positions.

A [2024 study using deepfakes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0276562424001057) confirmed that attractive applicants consistently scored higher on perceived competence and received more interview invitations, but the benefit was **gendered**: men benefited regardless of role type, while women only gained an advantage when applying for female-typed positions.

## What we do know about the US

The US lacks resume-photo audit studies, but it does have strong evidence on attractiveness bias in labor markets through other channels:

- **[Hamermesh & Biddle (1994)](https://ideas.repec.org/a/aea/aecrev/v84y1994i5p1174-94.html)**, published in the _American Economic Review_, used interviewer-rated attractiveness from national surveys and found a **5-10% wage penalty** for plain-looking workers compared to average-looking workers in the US, with effects at least as large for men as for women. The penalty existed across occupations, suggesting employer taste-based discrimination rather than productivity differences.
- **[Kee et al. (2023)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3709554)**, published in _Marketing Science_, analyzed 63,014 completed jobs and over 2 million applications on the US-based Freelancer.com platform. Freelancers who "looked the part" were significantly more likely to be hired, independent of demographics and beauty. There was **no correlation between "looking the part" and actual job performance**.
- **[Kline, Rose & Walters (2022)](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w29053/revisions/w29053.rev2.pdf)**, an NBER working paper, sent over **83,000 fictitious applications** to 108 of the largest US employers. Using names rather than photos to signal race, they found distinctively Black names reduced employer contact by 2.1 percentage points, with discrimination concentrated among a subset of firms.

The US evidence converges on the same conclusion through different methods: appearance-based signals (whether through photos, names, or in-person observation) trigger measurable bias in hiring. The specific mechanism of a photo on a resume just isn't testable in a market where the norm is to omit it.

**The takeaway**: photos introduce a variable you cannot control. Even if you're conventionally attractive, the benefit is gender-dependent and role-dependent. You're gambling on the demographics and biases of whoever screens your resume.

## The ethnicity and weight channels

Photos don't just signal attractiveness. They also reveal ethnicity, age, weight, and disability status, all of which trigger documented discrimination.

- The Italian study found that **non-native ethnicity was the strongest source of discrimination** in callbacks, more potent than gender or attractiveness alone.
- Weight-based discrimination was more severe for immigrants than natives, and gender gaps were larger among obese candidates ([Busetta et al.](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10888-020-09456-5)).
- France's experience with anonymous CVs (the _CV anonyme_ law of 2006, which required firms with 50+ employees to strip photos, names, age, and addresses from initial screening) found that applicants with migrant backgrounds received **40% fewer interview invitations** when their identity was visible.

Methodological reviews have raised the concern that when researchers use photos to test one characteristic (say, ethnicity), the photo simultaneously signals attractiveness, age, weight, and other traits, making it hard to isolate any single effect. This cuts both ways for job seekers: your photo is a bundle of signals, and you cannot predict which one the screener will react to.

## How industries differ

### Software and technology

**Do not include a photo.** This is the clearest norm across any industry.

- US Equal Employment Opportunity (EEO) laws discourage employers from viewing candidate photos, and many companies have formal policies to reject resumes with photos to avoid discrimination liability.
- **97.4% of Fortune 500 companies** use Applicant Tracking Systems (ATS), and most ATS software cannot parse images. A photo may cause your resume to be rejected before a human ever sees it.
- Silicon Valley culture explicitly values skill-based evaluation. A photo signals that you're unfamiliar with the norms.

### Finance and consulting

The picture is more complicated. **On your resume itself**, the same US/UK norms apply: no photo. But the finance industry places unusually high weight on **LinkedIn profile photos and personal presentation**.

- LinkedIn profiles with professional headshots receive **21x more profile views** and **36x more messages** than those without photos.
- **71% of recruiters** report rejecting candidates based on visual red flags on LinkedIn alone.
- In investment banking and Big Four consulting, the expectation is that your LinkedIn photo is polished, business-formal, and updated. The photo matters, just not on the resume document itself.

In continental European finance (particularly Germany and Switzerland), including a professional headshot on the CV remains common and sometimes expected, though this is slowly shifting.

### Creative and client-facing roles

Modeling, acting, real estate, fitness, and broadcast journalism are industries where a photo is expected or required. These are exceptions where physical appearance is a bona fide occupational qualification.

## Country norms

Geographic norms vary dramatically, and applying with the wrong convention signals unfamiliarity with the local market.

| Region                              | Photo norm            | Notes                                            |
| :---------------------------------- | :-------------------- | :----------------------------------------------- |
| **United States**                   | Do not include        | EEO laws, ATS rejection risk                     |
| **United Kingdom / Ireland**        | Do not include        | Equality Act discourages it                      |
| **Germany / Austria / Switzerland** | Common, declining     | Traditionally expected, shifting toward optional |
| **France**                          | Common, declining     | _CV anonyme_ law exists but is rarely enforced   |
| **Netherlands / Nordics**           | Optional, trending no | Increasingly seen as unnecessary                 |
| **Japan**                           | Expected              | Formal passport-style photo, conservative attire |
| **South Korea**                     | Expected              | Similar to Japan, high formality standards       |
| **China**                           | Expected              | Passport-sized, neutral background               |
| **Middle East**                     | Common                | Often expected, especially in Gulf states        |
| **Australia / New Zealand**         | Do not include        | Similar to US/UK norms                           |

A 2023 survey of Asia-Pacific recruiters found that **45% consider a photo a "nice-to-have"** while **12% say it directly influences shortlisting decisions**.

## The LinkedIn backdoor

Even in markets where resume photos are discouraged, recruiters routinely look up candidates on LinkedIn. This means the photo debate is partly theatrical: your appearance is one search away regardless.

The [Harvard Business School / USC study (2023)](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3709554), published in _Marketing Science_, analyzed 63,014 completed jobs and over 2 million applications on Freelancer.com. They found that freelancers who "look the part" for a job were significantly more likely to be hired, independent of demographics and attractiveness. Critically, there was **no correlation between "looking the part" and actual job performance**.

This creates an uncomfortable reality: bias enters through the profile photo channel even when it's absent from the resume.

## What this means for resume tools

Resume templates that include photo slots are making a design choice with real consequences. In the US, UK, and Australia, a photo slot actively encourages a practice that can trigger ATS rejection, legal liability for employers, and documented discrimination against candidates. In Japan, South Korea, and parts of continental Europe, omitting the photo slot creates friction for users who need one.

The pragmatic approach: make photo support available but not default. Don't render an empty placeholder when no photo is provided. Let the user opt in based on their target market rather than making them opt out of a norm that may not apply.
